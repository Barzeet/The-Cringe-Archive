00 — Theoretical Framework

What Is Cringe?

Cringe is often dismissed as a matter of bad taste — a joke, a scroll-past, a burst of collective laughter. This archive refuses that dismissal. Cringe is not in the video. It is in the social reaction to visible non-compliance. It emerges at the intersection of performance and judgment, affect and hierarchy.

When audiences mark content as "cringe," they are performing a social function: signaling that a norm has been violated. The discomfort is not aesthetic. It is political. It tells certain bodies and certain kinds of visibility: you should not be here, not like this, not as you are.

This archive takes cringe seriously as a sociological phenomenon — documenting not the performers as embarrassing subjects, but the social machinery of embarrassment itself.

"Cringe seems to be an affective and effective way of making the distinction between normal and non-normal deviance."
— Patrick Wöhrle, Two Shades of Cringe, Humanities (2021)
"Cringe is culture saying: 'You should have known better.' Refusal is the performer saying: 'I don't accept your terms.'"
— Archive Thesis
I
Cringe as Enforcement

Cringe marks moments when performance appears socially "out of place." Shame and secondhand embarrassment function as regulatory affects — disciplining the performer back into normative digital selfhood (Wöhrle, Smilges, Tomkins).

II
Cringe as Aesthetic Hierarchy

Digital culture relies on self-surveillance (Foucault / Greenblatt). Users are expected to anticipate judgment and adjust algorithmically. Cringe enforces who looks "naturally" at home online — mapping class, caste, and gender onto aesthetic competence.

III
Cringe as Cathartic Spectacle

Aristotle's catharsis in digital form: audiences gather to experience secondhand humiliation, releasing their own social anxieties through watching another perform "wrongly." Shame becomes shareable content with measurable return-visit rates.

IV
Cringe as Tiny Protest

The counter-reading this archive proposes: persistence in the face of ridicule — the continued posting — constitutes a form of refusal with political valence, however unintentional. These are tiny protests in the economy of visibility.

When cringe appears
Non-Compliance

The performer does not self-correct. The performance remains unapologetic. Posting continues at high frequency despite negative comment discourse and calls to report.

What cringe marks
The Out-of-Place

Non-elite interiors. Regional accents. Low-production aesthetics. Visible effort without polish. Bodies considered wrong for the medium. Age. Class. Queerness. Domestic labor made proudly public.

What cringe does
Norm Enforcement

Shame circulates socially (Ahmed). It attaches to certain bodies, accumulates through repetition, becomes "sticky." The archive documents this circulation — and the moments when performers metabolise the shame and post again anyway.

02 — Gender, Queerness & Refusal

Visibility Policed

Women's visibility is tightly policed online. Cringe often attaches when women appear too confident, too expressive, too present. The archive includes cis women, trans women, and queer love performances — forms of visibility that challenge normative gender and desire scripts in the Indian context.

Visibility Types in the Archive

Women in Domestic Space

Cooking, household labor, daily routine. Cringe attaches through class markers: open fires, modest interiors, absence of aesthetic polish.

Women Dancing / Being Joyful

Unapologetic public joy. The wide-armed dance, the open laugh, the defiant stare at camera — generates the strongest cringe-as-regulation response.

Trans Women in Public Space

Visibility in non-domestic spaces — petrol pumps, streets, markets. The archive documents violent comment discourse and communities of fierce affirmation.

Queer Love / WLW

Women-loving-women content navigates specific legal and social risks in India. Cringe reactions blend with moral condemnation, making visibility itself the act of protest.

Older Women as Performers

"Aunty" discourse is the archive's most common comment category — age-based shame applied to middle-aged women's digital ambition.

03 — Catharsis & Spectacle

The Economy of Watching

Cringe is also spectacular. Audiences gather to experience secondhand humiliation, collective embarrassment, emotional release. This creates an economy of watching where shame becomes shareable content — and the performer, despite the ridicule, generates sustained viewership that polished creators rarely achieve.

The Aristotle Problem

Aristotle's catharsis describes the purging of pity and fear through theatrical spectacle. Cringe viewing operates by analogous logic: audiences release social anxiety about their own digital performances by watching another person perform "wrongly." The creator becomes an unwitting therapeutic resource.

The Paradox of Return

The recurring comment pattern — "this was terrible, same time tomorrow?" — captures the fundamental paradox: audiences who claim to be repulsed return daily. The stated affect (cringe, disgust) and actual behavior (consumption, sharing) are precisely opposite. The archive reads this as evidence that cringe discourse is performed social regulation, not sincere emotion.

04 — Digital Humanities Methods

Method as Archival Practice

The archive is built through manual ethnographic observation, digital annotation, and computational text analysis. Each method generates a different layer: the performance, the audience reaction, and the structural pattern.

01
Reel Collection

Instagram Reels collected through platform observation, comment-section mining, and cringe-compilation tracking. Selection criteria: evidence of cringe comment discourse, circulation evidence, low assimilation to platform polish norms.

02
Metadata Annotation

Each entry manually annotated across seven axes: performance type, posting frequency, editing level, language, visible environment, comment tone, and circulation pattern. Annotation is interpretive, not algorithmic.

03
Comment Analysis

Comment sections documented as affective evidence. Comments coded across four types: correction, mockery, fascination, and solidarity. Voyant and Python/Pandas provide word frequency and thematic categorisation.

04
Circulation Tracing

Remix and imitation chains traced by following audio use, monitoring YouTube compilation videos, and tracking screenshot-based shares via reverse image search. Nine confirmed circulation chains documented.

Computational
Voyant Tools

Word frequency and corpus visualisation of the full comment dataset. Identifies dominant affective terms: "disgusting," "aunty," "properly," "women," "dance," "madam." Collocations reveal comment type clusters.

Computational
Python / Pandas

Thematic categorisation of comment types. DataFrame analysis of posting frequency distributions. Chi-square testing for correlation between creator class markers and comment hostility scores.

Computational
Embedding Analysis

Semantic analysis using word2vec to detect affective clustering in comment language. Identifies how "cringe" discourse groups around class, gender, and visibility themes without explicit category labeling.

06 — Appreciative Aesthetics

Towards an Appreciative Reading

Before asking whether something is cringe, the archive proposes five prior questions — a methodology that centres the performer's conditions rather than the audience's comfort.

What is this person trying to do?

What resources do they have — and what do they not have?

What risks are they taking to be visible in this space?

Who defined the right way to be visible online — and why do we accept that definition?

What happens to those who learn by trying in public, without a safety net of cultural capital?

The Archive Insists

I

That refusal is real. Even when unintentional. The continued posting — despite mockery, despite shame, despite social correction — is a form of presence that the archive takes seriously.

II

That visibility is political. Who gets to appear on screen without being labeled cringe is not a matter of aesthetics. It is a matter of class, gender, caste, region, and the unequal distribution of cultural capital.

III

That the archive record must be expanded. Most archives preserve successful creators and culturally validated expression. This archive preserves what is usually mocked, dismissed, scrolled past, erased.

"Cringe is evidence of people experimenting with digital selfhood under unequal conditions.
The archive asks you to watch again — differently."

07 — Significance

So What?

Short-form video is now one of the largest sites of public self-making globally. But only polished performances are algorithmically legitimised. Cringe performances reveal what that system costs.

✦
Aspiration Without Polish

The desire to be seen, to perform, to participate in digital culture — without the production values, lighting equipment, or social know-how that platforms algorithmically reward. Cringe is aspiration made visible in its most honest, unfiltered form.

✦
Effort Without Fluency

The visible effort in these performances — the trying — is cultural labor that the cringe label makes invisible. The archive restores this labor to visibility on its own terms, refusing the easy dismissal of the scroll-past.

✦
Visibility Without Safety Nets

Many creators post without knowing the risks — of mockery, harassment, or having their content aggregated in ridicule chains without consent. The archive documents this precarious visibility while refusing to reproduce the harm.

Dynamic network maps of remix and imitation chains — interactive, filterable by time, creator, and circulation type. Nodes will link to source content.

Planned
Longitudinal Comment Corpus

Time-series analysis of comment sentiment across individual creators' histories — tracking how cringe attribution evolves, intensifies, or transforms as a creator gains followers.

Research Phase
Creator Interviews

Oral history interviews with creators — centering their own understanding of their practice and their relationship to the platform.

Planned
Cross-Platform Comparison

Does cringe attribution function differently on Moj, Josh, Sharechat, and YouTube Shorts — platforms with different class and regional user distributions?

Technical
Full Searchable Database

A searchable database of the full comment corpus with annotation tags, filterable by creator, theme, sentiment, language, and circulation type.
